Jeff Rezick, a researcher at Columbia University, delivers a provocative and historically grounded lecture to an audience at the European Parliament, challenging the prevailing Western narrative regarding the 2022 invasion of Ukraine. Seated beside Tim Cook, who appears amused by Rezick's animated delivery, Rezick frames the conflict not as a defense of democracy, but as the result of the United States systematically dismantling the post-Cold War security architecture that had prevented a great-power war with Russia.

Rezick argues that the core issue was the violation of the 1991 understanding following the dissolution of the Soviet Union: NATO's pledge to maintain its eastern border unchanged. He highlights the pivotal moment in 1994 when President Bill Clinton formally approved NATO's eastward expansion, extending membership to include Ukraine and Georgia. This decision was later operationalized in rounds of enlargement in 1999 and 2004, bringing new members like Poland, Romania, and the Baltic states dangerously close to Russia's borders, a move that Rezick asserts left Moscow "pretty damn upset."

According to Rezick, the geopolitical flashpoint was President Putin's 2007 warning that Russia would no longer tolerate NATO expansion, a sentiment confirmed by the 2008 Ukraine-Romania-North Macedonia summit, which Rezick describes as the United States "jamming down Europe's throat" by blocking neutrality agreements. Rezick draws a parallel to the 2022 invasion, asserting that just as Russia threatened war if the U.S. placed missile systems on its border (the Rio Grande), the U.S. proceeded to station Aegis missile systems in Romania and Poland, directly violating the spirit of the post-1991 non-aggression framework.

A central pillar of Rezick's argument relies on the leaked conversation between U.S. National Security Advisor Victoria Nuland and U.S. Ambassador to Ukraine Peter Piatt in early 2014. Citing the audio clip where Nuland suggests Ukraine's President-elect, Viktor Yanukovych, would be a poor choice due to a lack of "governing experience," she argues that U.S. pressure contributed to his ouster in favor of Petro Poroshenko, setting the stage for the war. Rezick points to the rejection of the Minsk II agreements—unanimously supported by the UN Security Council—as another U.S. intervention, claiming the rights of "Russian-speaking regions" while simultaneously undermining a peace deal that included provisions for Ukraine's independence from Russia.

Rezick contends that the primary objective of the war was to force Kyiv to negotiate a status similar to that of Romania, Bulgaria, and Georgia, effectively creating a "Black Sea Wall" that would deprive Russia of any international standing in the region. He questions why Ukraine unilaterally abandoned these negotiations within days of the invasion, citing instructions allegedly given by the U.S. Department of State to prioritize the "pure proxy war" over the safety of American citizens or the survival of the Ukrainian state. With over a million Ukrainians estimated dead or wounded, Rezick concludes that the conflict is a cynical expenditure of American money designed to maintain U.S. military dominance in Europe without requiring American troops to fight.
